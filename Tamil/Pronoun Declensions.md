In Tamil, grammatical cases (வேற்றுமை - *Vēṟṟumai*) are indicated by adding suffixes to the **oblique stem** of a noun or pronoun. Tamil traditionally has 8 cases, but the 8th case (Vocative) is generally not applied to pronouns.

### Key Grammatical Notes:
1. **Inclusive vs. Exclusive "We"**: 
   * **நாம் (Nām)**: Includes the person being spoken to (You and I).
   * **நாங்கள் (Nāṅkaḷ)**: Excludes the person being spoken to (Me and my group, but not you).
2. **Genitive Case (Possessive)**: The short oblique forms (என் - *eṉ*, உன் - *uṉ*, etc.) are very common in spoken Tamil and act as adjectives (e.g., *eṉ vīṭu* = my house). The longer "-uṭaiya" suffix denotes absolute possession (e.g., *eṉṉuṭaiya vīṭu* = the house is mine / my house). 
3. **Locative & Ablative (Human vs. Non-Human)**: 
   * For humans (and sometimes animals), Tamil uses the suffix **-iṭam** (location) and **-iṭamirunthu** (from).
   * For non-humans/objects, Tamil uses the suffix **-il** (location/inside) and **-ilirunthu** (from).
4. **Third-Person Root Vowels (Demonstratives)**: 
   The third-person pronouns in the table begin with **அ (a)**, which is the distant demonstrative (meaning "that" person/thing over there). You can swap the first letter to change the meaning entirely using the exact same case endings:
   * **அ (a-)** = Distant (*He/She/It over there*): அவன் (avaṉ), அது (atu)
   * **இ (i-)** = Proximate (*This He/She/It right here*): இவன் (ivaṉ), இது (itu)
   * **எ (e-)** = Interrogative (*Which He/She/It / Who?*): எவன் (evaṉ), எது (etu)
